Culture of Sri Lanka

Population

23,103,565 (2024)
Over the last three years, Sri Lanka's population has grown gradually, with an average annual increase of about 0.25%. The growth rate has been slowing slightly, reflecting demographic transitions such as lower birth rates.

Language spoken

Sinhala (69%) and Tamil (25.2%) are the primary languages of most Sri Lankans. Other ethnic groups include the Malayalam, the Ambalavasi, the Mapilla, the Telugu, the Vaddars and the Waggai. Other languages have very few speakers (approximately just 300 people speak vedda). English is widely spoken.

Nations

Sri Lanka's beauty lies in its rich tapestry of diverse nations, where multiple cultures, languages, and religions coexist harmoniously. The population is composed of 74.9% Sinhalese, who are predominantly Buddhist, 15.3% Tamils, including 11.2% Sri Lankan Tamils and 4.1% Indian Tamils, mostly Hindus, 9.3% Moors, a Muslim community, and 0.5% Burghers, Malays, and others. This diversity fosters a unique blend of traditions, art, and cuisine, enhancing the island's cultural charm. These groups reflect the nation's rich ethnic and religious heritage, contributing to Sri Lanka's vibrant identity.

Religions

Sri Lanka is predominantly Buddhist, with 70.2% of the population practicing Buddhism, mainly among the Sinhalese. The island also has a significant Hindu population, making up 12.6%, mostly among Tamils, followed by 9.7% Muslims (Moors), and 7.4% Christians, including both Roman Catholics and Protestants. This religious diversity plays a vital role in shaping Sri Lanka’s cultural and social dynamics.

Holiday Schedule

‘Poya Day’ means ‘day of the full moon’: after Buddha was born, he experienced enlightenment and died on the day of the full moon of May (Vesak Poya). Therefore, all Poya Days are holidays in Sri Lanka: Buddhists dress up in white and visit temples to make offerings of flowers and incense and to meditate.

January: Thai Pongal.
February: Maha Shivaratri.
February 4: national holiday.
April: Avurudu.
April 13 and 14: New Year's Day.
May 1: Labour Day.
May 22: Hero's Day.
30 June: public holiday.
July or August: Perahera.
November: Diwali, the festival of lights.
December 25: Christmas.
December 31: non-working day.

History

Sri Lanka's history dates back over 2,500 years, with early kingdoms such as Anuradhapura and Polonnaruwa marking the island's cultural and political development. It was influenced by Buddhism from India, which became deeply ingrained in the society, and the island saw multiple invasions from South India. The colonial era began with the Portuguese in the 16th century, followed by the Dutch and finally the British, who ruled Sri Lanka until gaining independence in 1948.
Post-independence, Sri Lanka faced internal challenges, including ethnic tensions between the Sinhalese-majority and Tamil-minority, leading to a brutal civil war from 1983 to 2009. The war ended with the defeat of the Tamil Tigers, but its legacy continues to impact the nation. Today, Sri Lanka is a democratic republic, balancing its rich cultural heritage with modernization efforts.

Foods

Sri Lankan gastronomy is knockout, shaped with a superb natural larder (seafood, tropical fruits, and bold spices). In very simple terms, rice and curry are the basis of Sri Lankan cuisine, with the addition of meat or fish, various condiments and vegetables. Talking of condiments, sambol is incredibly popular – a delicious mish-mash of coconut flesh whizzed up with grated pepper and spices – often eaten with rice, or with bread for breakfast. Other popular dishes include Kiribath (rice cooked in coconut milk), served with a sprinkling of sesame seeds; cutlets (spicy croquettes) are deep-fried potato and fish balls; and biriyani is a Muslim specialty (rice, chicken or mutton or beef, thick with spices and topped with hard-boiled egg). For breakfast, go for the hoppers - rice pancakes made from rice flour and coconut milk, with a spicy side of dhal, curry and sambol. Vegetables and fruits are in abundance, too. Need something sweet? Order a memorable curd and treacle (buffalo milk curd with treacle).

Drinks

Sri Lanka offers a variety of refreshing drinks that reflect its cultural richness. Coconut water is a popular natural beverage, often enjoyed fresh from the coconut. Ceylon tea, globally renowned, is a staple in the country, served both hot and iced. Other local favorites include King Coconut water, a sweeter variety of coconut water, and Rasakinda, a sweet, spicy drink made from herbs and spices. The island also offers fruit-based drinks like Wood Apple juice and Lime juice, providing a burst of tropical flavors.
